# Benjamin Graham Value Investing Framework

## Who Is Benjamin Graham?

Benjamin Graham (1894–1976) is universally recognized as the father of value investing and security analysis. Born in London and raised in New York, Graham developed the first rigorous, systematic approach to analyzing securities based on their intrinsic value rather than market sentiment.

As a professor at Columbia Business School, Graham taught and mentored a generation of legendary investors, including Warren Buffett, Walter Schloss, Irving Kahn, and Tom Knapp. His two seminal works, "Security Analysis" (1934, co-authored with David Dodd) and "The Intelligent Investor" (1949), remain essential reading for serious investors nearly a century after publication.

Buffett has called "The Intelligent Investor" the best book on investing ever written, and Graham's influence extends to virtually every value-oriented investment firm in existence today.

## Core Principles

### Intrinsic Value

Graham's foundational concept is that every security has an intrinsic value that can be estimated through careful analysis of the company's assets, earnings, dividends, and financial strength. This intrinsic value may differ significantly from the market price, creating opportunities for disciplined investors.

The market price reflects the current consensus opinion about a company's worth, but consensus opinion is frequently wrong — influenced by emotion, herd behavior, and incomplete information. The value investor's job is to estimate intrinsic value independently and act when the market price diverges significantly from it.

### Margin of Safety

Graham's most important principle. The margin of safety is the difference between a security's intrinsic value and its market price. By buying only when this gap is substantial, investors protect themselves against errors in analysis, unforeseen events, and bad luck.

*"The function of the margin of safety is, in essence, that of rendering unnecessary an accurate estimate of the future."*

A 30-50% margin of safety was Graham's typical requirement. This discipline forces patience — you must wait for prices to fall far enough below intrinsic value before buying — but it dramatically reduces the risk of permanent capital loss.

### Mr. Market

Graham's famous allegory describes the stock market as a manic-depressive business partner named Mr. Market who shows up every day offering to buy your share of the business or sell you his. Some days Mr. Market is euphoric and offers absurdly high prices. Other days he is depressed and offers to sell at absurdly low prices.

The key insight: you are not obligated to trade with Mr. Market. You can take advantage of his irrational offers when they benefit you and ignore him the rest of the time. The market is there to serve you, not to instruct you.

*"The investor's chief problem — and even his worst enemy — is likely to be himself."*

### The Defensive vs. Enterprising Investor

Graham divided investors into two categories:

**The Defensive (Passive) Investor:**
- Wants safety and freedom from frequent decisions
- Buys a diversified portfolio of high-quality bonds and blue-chip stocks
- Rebalances annually between stocks and bonds (50-50 or adjusted based on valuations)
- Applies strict selection criteria: adequate size, strong financials, dividend history, moderate P/E ratios

**The Enterprising (Active) Investor:**
- Willing to devote significant time and effort to research
- Seeks bargains among undervalued stocks, special situations, and distressed securities
- Applies more aggressive valuation criteria and deeper analysis
- Must have the temperament to act contrary to the crowd

## Graham's Quantitative Criteria

Graham provided specific, measurable criteria for stock selection:

### For Defensive Investors
1. Adequate size (revenue > $100M in today's terms)
2. Strong financial condition (current assets at least 2x current liabilities)
3. Earnings stability (positive earnings for 10 consecutive years)
4. Dividend record (uninterrupted dividends for 20+ years)
5. Earnings growth (minimum 33% increase in per-share earnings over 10 years)
6. Moderate P/E ratio (not more than 15x average three-year earnings)
7. Moderate price-to-book ratio (not more than 1.5x book value)

### Net-Net Strategy
Graham's most conservative approach: buy stocks trading below their net current asset value (current assets minus total liabilities). This essentially means buying a dollar's worth of assets for less than 67 cents, providing an enormous margin of safety.

## How to Apply Graham's Framework

1. **Calculate intrinsic value** — Learn to estimate a company's worth based on assets, earnings power, and growth.
2. **Demand a margin of safety** — Never buy without a significant discount to intrinsic value.
3. **Ignore Mr. Market's mood** — Use market volatility as an opportunity, not a guide.
4. **Diversify adequately** — Graham recommended holding 10-30 stocks across different industries.
5. **Be patient and disciplined** — Value investing requires waiting for the right opportunities and acting decisively when they appear.
